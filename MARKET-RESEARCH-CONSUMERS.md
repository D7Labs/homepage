# Market Research Brief — Consumer-Side / Devotional Reader Demand

**Author:** Growth Marketer, D7 Labs
**Date:** 2026-05-05
**Companion to:** `MARKET-RESEARCH.md` (supply-side / pastor-tool competitive brief)
**Strategic question:** Should D7 publish a public devotional library to capture consumer search demand and funnel pastors back to the paid tool?

> **Headline / strategic call:** **CONDITIONAL YES — but not as a B2C devotional library.** A direct attempt to compete head-on with YouVersion (700M installs, 13M ratings) for consumer eyeballs is unwinnable for a brand-new domain on a 12-month horizon. The consumer-side opportunity is real, but the *winning shape* is not "build a public devotional library on d7labs.dev" — it is **(a) ship as a YouVersion content partner** (free distribution to a billion-install platform), **(b) publish a small, tightly-scoped `/devotionals/` lane on the homepage as link-bait + Pinterest-friendly artifact**, and **(c) capture pastors via the existing tool play, not consumer reach**. The traffic asymmetry is real — but the *intent* asymmetry is real too: a consumer reading a devotional is not 1 click from "let me try the pastor tool." The wedge is real but small. Don't bet the company on it.

**What "reading a devotional" looks like in 2026 — three things to internalize:**

1. **Mobile-first, app-not-web.** YouVersion's 1B installs and 19M-DAU peak make web-only devotional sites a long-tail surface. Web devotional reading is largely *email + Pinterest-driven inbound*, not a destination habit.
2. **Audio is the growth vector.** Glorify, Pray.com, Hallow, Dwell — all of the fast-growing apps are audio-first. Text-only is table stakes; audio is the differentiator.
3. **Sermon-derivation is already the dominant model — but humans are doing it.** Tony Evans, Charles Stanley, Alistair Begg, Joyce Meyer all repurpose broadcast sermons into daily devotionals via in-house editorial teams. **D7's tool is the automation layer for what these ministries already do manually.** That is a bigger insight than "consumers want devotionals."

### What the consumer SERPs teach us — three structural facts

1. **The "publish a famous pastor's devotional and rank for it" hypothesis is mostly broken.** `john piper devotional`, `tony evans devotional`, etc. are navigational queries. The pastor's official site (or the publishing house) wins by definition. The one third-party that ranked (sermons-online.org for Tony Evans) sits at #2-#4, not #1, and operates as a sermon-aggregation site, not as a tool.
2. **The "topical devotional" SERP (anxiety, grief, marriage) is owned by broadcast ministries who already do manually what D7 does automatically.** Truth For Life's "Anxiety's Antidote" *is* an Alistair Begg sermon turned devotional — by his editorial team. **D7's actual TAM here is selling our tool *to those ministries*, not competing with them on the consumer SERP.**
3. **The French Catholic + Protestant devotional web is more concentrated than English** — TopChrétien + AELF + Prions en Église own the lane in 5–10 sites. Lower volume; near-zero ranking opportunity for d7labs.dev. The **distribution play (TopChrétien partnership, YouVersion FR plan submission)** is more credible than the SEO play.

## 4. The "B2C wedge" hypothesis test

### Claim under test
> "If D7 published a sample devotional from John Piper's most-watched sermon, would that page have a chance to rank, and would it credibly funnel pastors back to the paid tool?"

### Evidence-based answer: **No to ranking; weak yes to funneling — but not in the shape the hypothesis assumes.**

**1. Ranking probability is low and structurally bounded.**
- For famous-pastor queries (`john piper devotional`, `tony evans devotional`) the SERP is **navigational**. Searchers want the pastor's own site or their publisher (Crossway, Desiring God). Google has a 15-year precedent of weighting brand-name + entity-graph signals here. A new domain at d7labs.dev cannot dislodge desiringgod.org from a "John Piper" query within 18 months under any realistic content+backlinks scenario.
- The one ranked third-party precedent — sermons-online.org for Tony Evans — sits at #2–#4, runs **dozens of pastor brands at once** (so it has site-wide authority), and dates to ~2010 ([archive evidence implied by URL structure and content volume](https://sermons-online.org/tony-evans/devo)). It took ~15 years to compound that authority. We don't have 15 years.
- Topical queries (`devotional for anxiety`, `devotional for grief`) are owned by broadcast ministries (Alistair Begg, Charles Stanley, Joyce Meyer) whose content is *already* sermon-derived. Competing with them on the SERP is competing with their 30+ years of broadcast brand and editorial reputation, not just their content.

**2. Domain Authority comparison (directional, no numerical claim).**
- [desiringgod.org](https://www.desiringgod.org/) — 2006 launch, John Piper's flagship, ~120K+ articles + sermons, deep referring-domain network from theology blogs, seminaries, Crossway publishing.
- [crossway.org](https://www.crossway.org/), [thegospelcoalition.org](https://www.thegospelcoalition.org/), [intouch.org](https://www.intouch.org/) — same tier.
- [d7labs.dev](https://d7labs.dev) — registered 2025, zero referring domains as of supply-side brief audit.
- I deliberately did not pull a numerical DA score (free Ahrefs/Moz tools rate-limit hard and would be one snapshot anyway). The qualitative gap is decisive: **for any query where Desiring God has even a marginal page, we lose for at least 12–18 months.**

**3. Copyright / legal — this is a real risk and should not be hand-waved.**

The user's flag was correct. Three layers:

- **Legal default for sermons:** Most pastor sermons are **work-for-hire owned by the church**, not the pastor personally, unless contracted otherwise ([Simms Showers LLP — IP and ownership](https://www.simmsshowerslaw.com/todays-sermon-is-brought-to-you-by-intellectual-property-and-ownership/), [Preaching.com — Who Owns Your Sermons](https://www.preaching.com/articles/who-owns-your-sermons/)). A YouTube-public sermon is *broadcast publicly* but is not in the public domain.
- **Fair use is narrow.** The four-factor test cuts against a commercial-aggregator publishing AI-derivative devotionals from a third-party preacher's sermon, especially when the result substitutes for a market the original publisher already sells in (Solid Joys, In Touch's daily devo, etc.) ([Christianity Today help article on fair use](https://help.christianitytoday.com/hc/en-us/articles/360048168033-How-do-you-know-whether-your-use-of-a-sermon-illustration-excerpt-story-or-quotation-is-a-permissible-fair-use); [Tripp Scott — copyright applies to non-profits](https://www.trippscott.com/insights/yes-copyright-laws-do-apply-to-churches-and-other-non-profits)). The U.S. Copyright Office's 2025 generative-AI report further notes that "where AI generated outputs are substantially similar to the training data inputs, there is a strong argument that … infringe[s] the reproduction and derivative work rights" ([Skadden summary](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2025/05/copyright-office-report); [Copyright Office Part 3 Report](https://www.copyright.gov/ai/Copyright-and-Artificial-Intelligence-Part-3-Generative-AI-Training-Report-Pre-Publication-Version.pdf)).
- **Practical reality:** Any sermon we'd want to publish from (Piper, Evans, Stanley, Begg) belongs to a ministry with a legal team and an existing devotional product. Publishing an AI-derived devotional from their content is a **cease-and-desist within weeks** outcome. Worst-case it is a brand-defining lawsuit; best-case it is a takedown that costs us the SEO equity we built.

**The only safe sermon sources are:** (a) sermons we generate ourselves as examples (synthetic), (b) sermons explicitly licensed to D7 by founding-church partners with written consent for public re-publication, (c) public-domain sermons (Spurgeon, Edwards, Wesley — copyright expired). Option (c) is interesting and underexplored in §5.

**4. Google Helpful Content Update / "thin AI" risk.**

Per [Search Engine Land coverage of the August 2024 + 2025 HCU iterations](https://searchengineland.com/library/platforms/google/google-algorithm-updates/helpful-content-update) and [Rankability's case study](https://www.rankability.com/data/does-google-penalize-ai-content/): sites with 80–90% unedited AI content saw **mass deindexing within 3–6 months** between late-2023 and mid-2025. Google's stance is "we don't penalize AI as such; we penalize unhelpful AI." A library of 100 LLM-generated devotionals on a brand-new domain with zero E-E-A-T signal is a textbook target.

**Counter-strategy if we publish anyway:** keep it small (≤25 pieces), add real editorial review (a real pastor's sign-off byline), include original analysis (the *why* of grounding, the *how* of D7's pipeline) above the generated artifact. Treat each devotional page as the lower half of a long article whose upper half is craft commentary.

## 5. Strategic recommendations

### The recommendation: **Conditional yes, but radically reshape what "yes" means.**

Do **not** build a public devotional library at `/devotionals/[slug]` populated from third-party sermons. The legal, ranking, and brand risk is high; the funnel from "consumer reads devo" to "pastor buys tool" is weak (a consumer reading on their phone is not your buyer); and the authority moat is decisive.

Do **build three smaller, asymmetric plays** that capture a compounded fraction of the same demand:

#### Play A — **Become a YouVersion content partner** (highest leverage, lowest risk)

- **What:** Submit 1–3 devotional plans to YouVersion Connect ([content partner program](https://www.youversion.com/content-partners), [submission guide](https://help.youversion.com/l/en/article/02sseuvj7f-submit-a-plan)). Free. ~14-day review.
- **Source content:** Generated from D7's *own* sample-sermons or licensed founding-church sermons — fully owned, fully legal.
- **Why this wins:** YouVersion has 1B+ installs and 7K+ existing partners. Even a mid-performing plan reaches *more readers in a month than a winning SEO post reaches in two years on a new domain.* And every plan can include "Created with D7 Engine — for pastors, see d7labs.dev/tools/sermon-to-devotional" as an attribution byline. **This is the pastor funnel.** Pastors browsing YouVersion plans see "Generated with D7" and click through.
- **Cost:** ~10 hours per plan, including writing the partnership-pitch deck.
- **Metrics that matter:** Plan completion rate, byline click-through to d7labs.dev, downstream tool signups attributable to YouVersion.

#### Play B — **A small public showcase library, scoped to risk** (homepage SEO + Pinterest)

- **What:** Publish **5–10 sample devotionals** at `/devotionals/[slug]`, each one:
  - Generated from a **public-domain sermon** (Spurgeon, Edwards, Wesley, Whitefield) OR a **D7 founding-church sermon with written consent**. *Never* a third-party copyrighted sermon.
  - Wrapped in a long-form article that includes: the original sermon excerpt and link, the generated devotional artifact, **a paragraph by a real D7 team member explaining the grounding methodology** (E-E-A-T signal), and a Pinterest-portrait preview image.
  - Each page targets a long-tail topic (`devotional from spurgeon's sermon on anxiety`, `wesley sermon turned 3-day devotional`).
- **Why this wins:** Public-domain sermons have **zero copyright risk**, no entity-graph competition (Spurgeon is dead; he's not ranking), and the long-form wrapper passes Helpful-Content-Update muster. It's also the only credible way to publish without legal exposure.
- **Cost:** ~3 hours per devotional + design ~5 hours.
- **Metrics:** Organic impressions in GSC, Pinterest pin saves, time-on-page > 2 min, CTR from page to `/tools/sermon-to-devotional`.

#### Play C — **Pastoral SEO co-marketing** (link this to supply-side brief)

- **What:** Pitch Tony Evans / Charles Stanley / Joyce Meyer / Truth For Life ministries to use D7 to *automate the work they're already doing manually*. We are not the consumer brand; we are the **automation infrastructure** for ministries that already publish sermon-derived devotionals.
- **Why this wins:** Reframes the legal risk inside-out. If In Touch Ministries uses D7 to generate Charles Stanley devos, the IP belongs to In Touch, who already owns the sermons. We capture margin from broadcast ministries that have 30 years of brand authority on the consumer SERP. We don't compete with them; we power them.
- **Cost:** Outreach + sales motion. ~20 hours/month for 3 months to land first ministry partnership.
- **Metrics:** Number of ministry partnerships, monthly devotional volume processed via tool.

### What I'd skip / deprioritize

- **A standalone consumer brand (Ezra Studio's consumer wing).** Memory says Ezra is the consumer-product surface; that's a multi-quarter, multi-year app build. The supply-side brief's Phase 1 priority is correctly the pastor tool. **Do not bend the 30-day plan toward consumer SEO; it dilutes the tool's GTM.**
- **French-language consumer SEO at d7labs.dev.** TopChrétien + AELF + Prions own the lane. The FR play is **Play C with TopChrétien as the partner** (or with French-Canadian church networks per memory `project_language_priority`).

### Interaction with Ezra Studio

Ezra Studio (per memory `project_brand_architecture`) is the *creator* surface and Ezra (consumer) is the consumer surface. **Plays A + B build domain authority for d7labs.dev that benefits both products** without forking marketing into a "Ezra consumer launch" before product readiness. ## Addendum — AI Search lens

Applied after-the-fact through the `ai-seo` skill. Two strategic implications change the original recommendations:

### 1. Google AI Overviews are eating devotional consumer SERPs

The skill flags that **AI Overviews appear in ~45% of Google searches and reduce clicks to ranked sites by up to 58%**. For consumer queries like *"devotional for anxiety"*, *"what is a devotional"*, and *"daily devotional for marriage"* — even ranking #1 organically may not deliver clicks. The actual battle is **getting cited as a source inside the AI Overview**, which favors:

- Direct definition in the first paragraph
- Self-contained 40–60 word answer blocks
- Cited statistics with sources
- Expert quotes with attribution
- Recently-updated content with visible "last updated" date

This means the showcase library (Play 2) needs each devotional page wrapped in **AI-extractable commentary** — not just the devotional text alone. Per the Princeton GEO research the skill cites, *adding statistics gives a 37% citation boost; adding citations gives 40%; adding expert quotes gives 30%.* Cumulative if combined.

### 2. YouVersion is invisible to AI

YouVersion's Bible Plans are **walled-garden mobile content**. ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews can't read inside the app. So Play A (YouVersion partnership) delivers human reach but **zero AI citation lift**. The consumer brief's recommendation to lead with YouVersion still stands — but frame it as *human distribution*, not *search/AI distribution*. They're complementary, not substitutes.

Per the AI SEO skill's "Pillar 3: Presence — Be Where AI Looks":

### How to write the `/learn/*` pages so they get cited

Each content page from the supply-side brief's §4 must follow the GEO recipe:

1. **Lead with a definition in the first paragraph** that answers the page's target query in 40–60 words. AI Overviews extract this verbatim.
2. **Use H2s phrased exactly like search queries** ("How long does it take to turn a sermon into a devotional?" not "Time required").
